# Audience & ICP

## One-sentence ICP

*People who make a lot of commitments but don't have the systems or discipline to track them — and don't want another system to manage.*

---

## The Audience

**Small business operators with a hustle mentality.**

| Characteristic | Detail |
|---------------|--------|
| Team size | 2–10 people |
| Work style | Hustle — doing the work of 100 with 10 |
| Tracking method | Working memory, not task systems |
| Logging discipline | Low — they just get things done |
| Forgetting pattern | Natural consequence of volume, not incompetence |
| Preference | Don't want another system — want a safety net |

---

## Not the Target User

Corporate knowledge workers in structured environments where:
- Loop-closing is a trained, supported behavior
- Dedicated systems exist for tracking (CRMs, project tools, dedicated ops staff)
- Teams have budget and time for process
- Accountability is enforced organizationally

**Why this matters for product decisions:** When a feature makes sense for a corporate user but not for a small business operator, build for the small business operator.

---

## The Core Problem

People at this scale:
- Make verbal commitments in meetings that never get written down
- Send emails with action items that get buried in threads
- Delegate in Slack messages that nobody tracks
- Work from working memory until it fails them

The failure mode isn't laziness — it's volume without infrastructure.

---

## What Rippled Is (and Isn't) For This Audience

**Is:** A quiet safety net that catches what's falling through

**Is not:** A comprehensive task management system — they already have enough systems

**The key interaction:** Rippled occasionally says "you said you'd do this, I didn't see it get done, you may want to look at that." That's it. Subtly.

---

## Insight from Kevin's Conversation with Minnie (2026-03-17)

Minnie comes from a corporate environment where loop-closing is a trained, supported behavior. Her instinct was "shouldn't the system do everything?" — which is the right question for her world.

Kevin's instinct — "we live between the systems, not on top of them" — is the right answer for the Rippled audience.

That tension surfaces a useful design test: **if a feature would make Minnie's corporate team more productive, but would overwhelm Matt's 10-person insurance agency, build for Matt.**
